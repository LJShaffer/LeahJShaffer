About

Bridging worlds,
one landscape at a time

I am a PhD candidate in Sustainability at Arizona State University, where my work sits at the intersection of governance, ecology, and justice in the American Arctic.

My dissertation — Navigating governance and livelihood dynamics across Alaska's landscapes and seascapes — investigates how the worldviews held by communities, agencies, and Tribal nations shape the decisions that affect frozen landscapes and coastal fisheries in Alaska. At its core, this work asks: when people who understand the world in fundamentally different ways sit at the same table, what happens?

I blend qualitative methods (semi-structured interviews, archival document analysis, fuzzy cognitive mapping) with quantitative approaches (difference-in-difference regression, semantic network analysis) to produce research that is useful not only to scholars, but to the Indigenous communities and land managers who navigate these governance challenges every day.

Before beginning my PhD, I worked in food security, riparian restoration, and Indigenous water sovereignty — commitments that continue to ground my scholarship in place-based realities.

Research

Three studies,
one dissertation

My doctoral research is structured around three interconnected studies, each examining a different aspect of livelihoods and natural resource stewardship in Alaska — from place-based community decision-making to broad federal fisheries policy.

01
Study 1 · Qualitative · NSF-Funded

Worldviews in Frozen Commons Governance

How do differing worldviews held by multi-level stakeholders — from Tribal councils to federal agencies — affect the care and management of Alaska's frozen landscapes under climate change? Working in the Upper Kuskokwim Region with the communities of McGrath and Nikolai, I use semantic network analysis of government documents and fuzzy cognitive mapping of semi-structured interviews to document the universe of worldviews shaping frozen-commons governance, and trace their effects on conflict, consensus, and decision-making across jurisdictional levels. This research is funded by a National Science Foundation Doctoral Dissertation Research Improvement Grant ($62,194).

02
Study 2 · Community Co-Design · Theory Building

Co-Creating a Theory of Worldviews in Social-Ecological Systems

Building on findings from Chapter 2, I work with McGrath and Nikolai Village Tribal Councils to co-create a theory of how worldviews operate within multi-level governance and social-ecological system (SES) frameworks. Through community workshops, we develop frozen-commons stewardship scenarios and investigate likely futures — examining tradeoffs in livelihoods, landscape navigation, cultural connection, and ecosystems that result when decision-makers with different worldviews shape policy. This chapter centers Indigenous knowledge as an equal partner in theory-building, not merely a subject of study.

03
Study 3 · Quantitative · Walton Family Foundation

Catch Share Management & Community Development in Coastal Alaska

Marine fisheries are a lifeline for Alaska's coastal communities — and for Indigenous peoples, they are woven into cultural identity and sovereignty. This chapter uses a difference-in-difference regression model to measure how sixteen Alaskan villages are affected when commercial fisheries transition from race-to-fish management to catch share allocation. By combining NOAA fishery engagement data, NASA nighttime lights, and business establishment records, I construct a novel dataset to ask: does catch share management distribute economic benefits equitably — and what does that mean for Alaska Native communities?

Stakeholder Engagement

Research that serves
communities first

A core goal of my work is to elevate community perspectives. I maintain ongoing relationships with Tribal councils, community partners, and land managers in the Upper Kuskokwim Region — and I design research that community members have explicitly said will help them navigate governance conversations and advocate for their voices in multi-level decision-making.

🏔️

Indigenous Community Partnerships

I work directly with McGrath and Nikolai Village Tribal Councils as co-designers of the research. Community partners guide research questions, co-identify inclusion criteria, and are consulted before every major project decision. Research findings will be returned to communities in accessible formats to support their long-term decision-making on their own terms.

🌊

Tribal Water Sovereignty

As an Alaska Conservation Foundation intern with the Yukon River Inter-Tribal Watershed Council (2022), I investigated federal and state water conservation law to design a road map toward Indigenous water sovereignty in the Yukon River Basin — bridging legal complexity with Tribal stakeholder knowledge and priorities.

🐟

Food Security & Livelihood Programs

As an AmeriCorps VISTA with the Fairbanks Soil and Water Conservation District (2019-2020), I led foundational design of a farmer training program connecting grassroots nonprofits to Tribal and university partners — and leading a team of collaborators to successfully secure a $375,213 USDA Community Food Projects grant to support the program.

🌱

Riparian Restoration & Youth Education

As Program Coordinator for Youth for Habitat (2021), I led student groups in designing and implementing riparian restoration projects in critical salmon habitat around Fairbanks — combining hands-on conservation with field-based ecological education for Alaskan youth.

🎓

Undergraduate Mentorship

I mentor undergraduate researchers through ASU's Sustainability Undergraduate Research Experience and Research Apprenticeship Program — earning the Excellence in Mentorship Award (2025) for my commitment to supporting emerging researchers.

📢

Public Science Communication

I present research in public and practitioner-facing venues including the ASU Institute for Social Science Research, the 20th Biennial Conference of the International Association for the Study of the Commons, the ASU School of Sustainability Seminar Series, and the Navigating the New Arctic Annual Community Meeting.
